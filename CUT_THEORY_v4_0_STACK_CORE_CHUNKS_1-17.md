## Chunk 1 — CANON: PRIMITIVES & OPERATORS (v4.0) <a id="chunk-1"></a>

### 0 — PURPOSE + CONTRACT

**0.1 — Purpose**
Present a minimal ontology that generates structure, change, and layered emergence from a small set of primitives and operators.

**0.2 — Contract**
This document is a conceptual engine, not a claim that nature “must” be this way.
Physical mappings come later as hypotheses and test-bridges.

**0.3 — Style rule**
Every term must be either:
- primitive (not defined in terms of other canon terms), or
- derived (explicitly constructed from earlier terms).

---

### 1 — PRIMITIVES (ATOMIC DEFINITIONS)

**P1 — DISTINCTION**
A distinction is the minimal act that separates “this” from “not-this”.

**P2 — IDENTITY**
Identity is a distinction that can be recognized as the same across transitions.

**P3 — BOUNDARY**
A boundary is a stabilized distinction that enforces inside/outside as a categorical separation.

**P4 — RECURRENCE**
Recurrence is the re-appearance of an identity (or boundary-pattern) across transitions.

**P5 — COHERENCE**
Coherence is the capacity for a pattern to persist through change without dissolving into unrelated fragments.

---

### 2 — OPERATORS (ATOMIC ACTIONS)

**O1 — CUT**
A cut is the operation that creates a new boundary (thereby creating sides).

**O2 — SLICE**
A slice is an operation that acts on an existing boundary: it collapses, redirects, or reconfigures that boundary.

**O3 — UPDATE**
An update is one step of allowable transition: the moment recurrence either holds or fails.

---

### 3 — IMMEDIATE DERIVATIONS

**D1 — SIDES**
A cut produces sides: inside vs outside.
“Sides” are not spatial; they are categorical partitions.

**D2 — TENSION**
Tension is the relational pressure introduced by a boundary because two sides are now non-equivalent.
Tension is not a force; it’s the demand for resolution introduced by enforced difference.

**D3 — STABILITY**
Stability is coherence maintained over many updates: tension stays within tolerance and recurrence stays recognizable.

**D4 — FABRIC**
A fabric is a network of mutually reinforcing recurrences (many identities stabilizing each other).

**D5 — DIMENSION**
A dimension is a recurrence-pattern that remains coherent under all valid slices available at that level.

**D6 — AGENCY**
Agency is selective recurrence: the active preservation of identity by choosing among available slice-paths.

---

### 4 — THE CORE ASYMMETRY (CUT vs SLICE)

**4.1 — Cut creates sides**
Cut introduces a boundary where none existed.

**4.2 — Slice transforms sides**
Slice operates only where a boundary already exists.
Slice is “change” without requiring new primitives: it is structure re-written, not structure created from nothing.

**4.3 — Why this matters**
If you merge Cut and Slice, you lose the clean separation between:
- world-building (new distinctions), and
- world-updating (reconfiguration of existing distinctions).

---

### 5 — NECESSITY CHAIN (THE ENGINE)

CUT → BOUNDARY → SIDES → TENSION → RECURRENCE → FABRIC → DIMENSION → IDENTITY-RICH WORLDS → AGENCY

Each arrow is: “given the prior term, the next becomes unavoidable for persistence.”

---

### 6 — QUICK CHECKS (CANON GUARDRAILS)

**6.1 — Nothing is not a thing in the canon**
“Nothing” is the limit case: absence of identity/recurrence, not an entity.

**6.2 — No hidden physics**
Words like space, time, particle, field do not appear here as primitives.
They arrive later as mappings from fabric/dimension behavior.

**6.3 — No extra metaphysics**
If a claim can’t be built from the above, it belongs in hypotheses, not in the canon.

## Chunk 2 — CANON: THE LOGIC OF SLICES + THE BIRTH OF DIMENSIONS (v4.0) <a id="chunk-2"></a>

### 1 — SLICE LOGIC (CLEAN DEFINITIONS)

**1.1 — Slice acts on boundary**
A slice cannot operate on undividedness.
It only operates where a cut has already produced a boundary.

**1.2 — Slice outcomes (the 3 outcomes)**
A slice event rewrites adjacency and produces one of:

A) Preserved Identity
The boundary survives, gains complexity, and recurrence tightens.

B) Shifted Identity
The boundary persists but reorients (domain or alignment changes).

C) Divided Identity
The boundary loses single-coherence and splits into multiple coherences.

**1.3 — Slice is a coherence-test**
A slice is the moment a structure is forced to prove it can re-form.
If it re-forms → coherence deepens.
If it fails → collapse to earlier recurrence.

**1.4 — Slice does not erase distinctions**
A slice dissolves closure, not “everything.”
Pieces persist but lose their previous loop-coherence.

---

### 2 — TENSION → SLICE THRESHOLDS

**2.1 — Tension gradients**
Tension can be smooth (distributed) or sharp (localized).

**2.2 — Two regimes**
A) Distributed tension → refinement
When tension spreads evenly, recurrence becomes smoother and more stable.

B) Concentrated tension → slicing
When tension concentrates beyond tolerance, a slice event occurs.

**2.3 — The universal transition rule**
When tension exceeds boundary capacity, a slice reconfigures structure along the path of least destabilization.

---

### 3 — LOOP FORMATION (FIRST IDENTITIES)

**3.1 — Loop definition**
A loop is a boundary that closes in on itself and can re-instantiate across updates.

**3.2 — Why loops matter**
A line has ends (instability points).
A loop distributes tension across closure and can persist without external reference.

**3.3 — Slice-survival requirement**
A loop becomes a real identity only once it survives slicing without dissolving into incoherent fragments.

---

### 4 — DIAGONAL PRINCIPLE (THE ENGINE OF NEW LAYERS)

**4.1 — Symmetry cannot hold indefinitely**
Perfect symmetry is fragile because tension makes differences consequential.

**4.2 — Diagonal definition**
A diagonal is a stable deviation from symmetry that does not collapse the system that produced it.

**4.3 — Diagonals are pre-dimensions**
A diagonal is the first sign that recurrence has escaped its original frame.

Diagonals that survive become new rules.
New rules become new degrees of freedom.

---

### 5 — DIMENSIONAL ACCUMULATION

**5.1 — Dimension is not a container**
A dimension is not “where things sit.”
A dimension is a stabilized variable: a recurrence-pattern that stays coherent under slicing.

**5.2 — How dimensions form**
Repeated slices with consistent bias create a directional lean.
Lean that persists becomes an axis.
Axes that co-stabilize become a dimensioned fabric.

**5.3 — Density without space**
Density = recurrence-per-update (coherence thickness), not “stuff per volume.”

High coherence thickness → robust structures.
Low coherence thickness → flicker, drift, collapse.

**5.4 — Threshold rule**
A dimension appears only once slice-to-slice similarity becomes self-supporting.

Before threshold:
- patterns flicker
- diagonals collapse
- symmetry breaks too fast

After threshold:
- recurrences lock
- axes stabilize
- invariants persist

---

### 6 — PROTO-TEMPORALITY (TIME WITHOUT MAKING IT A DIMENSION)

**6.1 — Time is not primitive**
Time is the ordering of coherent updates.

**6.2 — Arrow as gradient**
Forward = direction of least destabilization (easy continuation).
Backward = requires near-perfect reconstruction (almost impossible).

So the arrow is not commanded — it emerges from asymmetry + stability economics.

**6.3 — Branching as compression failure**
When coherence cannot collapse competing continuations into one, multiple continuations persist.
Probability is relational weight over those continuations.

---

### 7 — CANON SUMMARY (ONE PARAGRAPH)

Cuts create boundaries; boundaries create tension; tension either refines recurrence or triggers slice events. Slice events test coherence and rewrite adjacency, producing preserved, shifted, or divided identity. Loops are the first stable identities because closure distributes tension. Symmetry breaks under tension, producing diagonals; diagonals that survive become new degrees of freedom. Dimensions accumulate as stabilized recurrence-patterns that remain coherent under slicing. Time is not a dimension but the ordered inheritance of coherent updates; branching occurs when collapse cannot compress alternatives into one continuation.

## Chunk 3 — PHYSICAL INTERFACE: FROM FABRIC TO PHYSICS (v4.0) <a id="chunk-3"></a>

### 1 — WHAT “PHYSICAL” MEANS IN THIS STACK
Physical reality is the subset of the fabric where:
- recurrences become measurable invariants
- slice-events become repeatable interactions
- adjacency becomes stable enough to act like space

Physics is boundary logic interpreted through stable measurement.

---

### 2 — LOCALITY & SPACE (EMERGENT GEOMETRY)

**2.1 — Adjacency**
Adjacency is not location.
Adjacency is “ease of transition” between two identity-states.

**2.2 — Distance (operational)**
Define distance as difficulty of transition:

d(i,j) := -log(w(i,j))

where w(i,j) is the transition weight (how easy i→j is).

High transition weight → short distance.
Low transition weight → long distance.

**2.3 — Geometry**
Geometry is the path-structure induced by adjacency:

D(a,b) := min over paths γ from a to b of Σ d(i,j) along γ.

Space is the stabilized adjacency graph.

---

### 3 — FIELDS, PARTICLES, AND “MATTER”

**3.1 — Field**
A field is a smooth pattern across adjacent regions:

φ is a field if |φ(i) - φ(j)| is small for adjacent i,j.

Fields are “slow-varying recurrence features.”

**3.2 — Particle**
A particle is a localized recurrence-dense mode:
- concentrated persistence in a small adjacency neighborhood
- re-instantiated across many slices

Particles are not objects.
They are stable local invariants of the fabric.

**3.3 — Mass (structural)**
Mass is resistance to reconfiguration:
- how much a pattern must be rewritten to shift its identity
- “stiffness” of a localized mode under slicing

---

### 4 — FORCES AS GRADIENT RULES

**4.1 — Force (core definition)**
A force is the directional tendency produced by uneven tension in adjacency.

**4.2 — Gradient form**
Where a field varies, transitions bias:

F ∝ -∇(tension / potential-pattern)

“Motion” is the path that preserves coherence with least destabilization cost.

**4.3 — Curvature / gravity analogue**
If local transition weights distort, shortest paths bend.
Curvature is non-uniform adjacency.

Gravity-like behavior appears when:
- recurrence density distorts adjacency
- paths curve toward regions that alter transition structure

---

### 5 — QUANTUM AS SLICE-SET LOGIC (NO NEW PRIMITIVES)

**5.1 — Superposition**
Superposition = identity not yet locked to one cut outcome.
It is an accessible set of coherent continuations.

**5.2 — Measurement**
Measurement = applying a stabilizing cut + recording which continuation persists.
Collapse = boundary lock-in.

**5.3 — Interference**
Interference occurs when multiple coherent paths contribute before lock-in:
- some paths reinforce
- some cancel

**5.4 — Entanglement**
Entanglement = shared recurrence constraints across separated regions:
two identities inherit one coherence-rule-set.

Nonlocal-looking effects come from global coherence constraints, not “signals.”

---

### 6 — THERMODYNAMICS & THE ARROW

**6.1 — Entropy (structural)**
Entropy = loss of sharp distinctions under uncontrolled slicing.
More slicing pathways → more blurred identity.

**6.2 — Equilibrium**
Equilibrium = tension smoothing across the fabric:
- distinctions stop producing net directional pressure
- local gradients flatten

**6.3 — Arrow of time**
The arrow is the net direction where:
- reconstruction becomes harder
- information about prior micro-structure is lost under coarse recurrence

Forward is easy continuation.
Backward requires near-perfect restoration.

---

### 7 — RELATIVITY (AS FABRIC-UPDATE EFFECTS)

**7.1 — Time (again, operational)**
Time is the ordering of coherent updates.

**7.2 — Time dilation (structural)**
When an identity must spend more recurrence “budget” maintaining coherence under stress or motion through adjacency, fewer updates remain for internal clocks.
Different traversal conditions → different experienced update rates.

**7.3 — Speed limits**
There is a maximum stable propagation rate set by:
- how fast coherence can transfer across adjacency without fragmenting identity

c is an emergent stability limit of slice-to-slice propagation.

---

### 8 — ONE-LINE TRANSLATION TABLE (FOR WRITING + TEACHING)

Cut → distinction event
Boundary → stable partition
Tension → imbalance / pressure for reconfiguration
Slice → boundary rewrite event
Fabric → network of recurrences
Dimension → invariant recurrence-pattern stable under slicing
Field → smooth fabric feature
Particle → localized persistent mode
Force → gradient-driven bias in transitions
Space → stabilized adjacency geometry
Time → ordered update chain
Entropy → distinction blur under uncontrolled slicing

## Chunk 4 — COGNITIVE INTERFACE: MIND AS SLICE NAVIGATION (v4.0) <a id="chunk-4"></a>

### 1 — WHAT “MIND” MEANS IN THIS STACK
Mind is what happens when a fabric becomes:
- self-referential (it models itself)
- predictive (it simulates continuations)
- selective (it chooses which continuations to reinforce)

Mind is not “stuff.”
Mind is a high-order identity that survives by steering its own recurrences.

---

### 2 — THE INTERNAL WORLD: MODELS AS BOUNDARIES

**2.1 — Concept**
A concept is an internal boundary:
- it separates “counts as X” from “doesn’t count as X”
- it compresses many differences into one stable distinction

**2.2 — Belief / assumption**
A belief is a boundary the system treats as low-cost to reuse.
It’s a recurrence shortcut.

**2.3 — Meaning**
Meaning is tension inside a model:
- when a distinction matters to continuation
- when “this vs that” changes what the system should do next

Meaning = actionable difference.

---

### 3 — PERCEPTION: CUTS APPLIED TO THE WORLD

**3.1 — Sensing**
Sensing is repeated cutting of incoming flux into stable categories.

**3.2 — Percept**
A percept is a cut outcome that survives long enough to be re-used.

**3.3 — Illusion (operational)**
Illusion is a model-boundary that outlasts the evidence that should have sliced it.
Not “wrongness” — persistence past its usefulness.

---

### 4 — ATTENTION: TENSION PRIORITIZATION

Attention is not a spotlight.
Attention is a tension budget allocator.

**4.1 — Attention rule**
The system prioritizes distinctions that:
- reduce future destabilization
- protect identity continuity
- preserve valuable invariants

**4.2 — Distraction**
Distraction is competing tension gradients pulling selection sideways.

**4.3 — Focus**
Focus is coherent recurrence: the same boundary is reused across many updates.

---

### 5 — MEMORY: RECURRENCE WITHIN THE SELF

**5.1 — Memory (core)**
Memory is not storage.
Memory is preferred reconstruction.

A system “remembers” when it rebuilds the same boundary-pattern reliably.

**5.2 — Learning**
Learning is boundary refinement:
- successful boundaries get cheaper to reuse
- failed boundaries get sliced, reshaped, or dropped

**5.3 — Trauma (structural)**
Trauma is a high-tension imprint that forces recurrence even when it harms coherence.
It’s a loop that got too expensive to rewrite.

---

### 6 — THOUGHT: SLICE-SIMULATION

Thought is offline slicing:
- simulate possible continuations
- estimate tension outcomes
- pick the continuation with highest coherence payoff

**6.1 — Imagination**
Imagination is simulated cut-application without committing to a single slice chain.

**6.2 — Reasoning**
Reasoning is constraint-preserving simulation:
keep invariants stable while exploring alternatives.

**6.3 — Insight**
Insight is diagonalization in cognition:
a stable new perspective emerges because old recurrences couldn’t reconcile tension.

---

### 7 — QUALIA: RESONANCE SIGNATURES OF INTERNAL STATES
Qualia are not “mystical particles.”
Qualia are the felt signature of:
- a boundary-state
- inside an identity
- as tension redistributes through the internal fabric

Different internal configurations produce different resonance patterns.
Those patterns are what it is like.

(Operational claim: felt character tracks the system’s internal coherence geometry.)

---

### 8 — THE SELF: LONG-RANGE IDENTITY COHERENCE

**8.1 — Self-model**
The self is the boundary bundle the system uses to predict its own continuations.

**8.2 — Identity continuity**
A self exists if:
- the system can keep a stable “me” boundary across many slice events
- and can correct drift without collapsing

**8.3 — Ego failure modes**
- Shatter: tension overwhelms coherence → fragmentation
- Drift: recurrences misalign over time → “not myself”
- Corruption: core invariants distort → unstable self-narrative

---

### 9 — AGENCY: SELECTIVE RECURRENCE AS CONTROL

Agency is not magic freedom.
Agency is selection under constraint.

**9.1 — Minimal agency requirements**
- memory (preferred reconstruction)
- prediction (slice-simulation)
- valuation (tension shaping: what matters)
- action capacity (ability to alter future cuts/slices)
- identity stability (a self to preserve)

**9.2 — Choice (operational)**
Choice is selecting a continuation that:
- preserves identity
- manages tension
- maintains coherence with the least destabilization cost

**9.3 — Free will (in this frame)**
“Free” = not externally forced in the immediate step.
“Will” = internal selection rule based on identity + valuation.

It’s real as a process, constrained as a structure.

## Chunk 5 — COMPUTATIONAL INTERFACE: FORMAL SYSTEMS AS CUT-SEQUENCES (v4.0) <a id="chunk-5"></a>
1 — WHY COMPUTATION BELONGS IN THE CANON

Computation is not “separate from reality.”
Computation is what recurrence looks like when:
    - distinctions are discrete
    - transitions are rule-governed
    - identity is tracked step-by-step

A computer is a fabric that forces slice order to be explicit.
---
2 — THE MINIMAL COMPUTATIONAL CORE

2.1 — State
State is a momentary configuration of distinctions.

2.2 — Update rule
An update rule is a slice operator on state:
S: state_t → state_{t+1}

2.3 — Program
A program is a stabilized recurrence of update rules.

2.4 — Execution
Execution is traversal: repeated application of slices under a fixed recurrence scheme.
---
3 — DATA STRUCTURES AS BOUNDARIES

3.1 — Data structure
A data structure is a boundary arrangement that makes certain recurrences cheap:
    - arrays privilege adjacency
    - trees privilege hierarchy
    - graphs privilege relationship navigation

3.2 — Type
A type is a category boundary that restricts valid slices.

3.3 — Interface
An interface is a permitted cut-set: what distinctions you may apply to a structure without collapsing it.
---
4 — LOGIC AS CUT COMPOSITION

4.1 — Boolean logic
A boolean value is a two-sided distinction.
AND/OR/NOT are compositions of cuts over shared identity.

4.2 — Predicates
A predicate is a cut on a domain: it partitions “satisfies” vs “does not satisfy.”

4.3 — Proof (operational)
A proof is a slice chain that preserves invariants under every allowed transformation.

Correctness = invariant survival.
---
5 — ERROR, BUGS, AND TENSION

5.1 — Error (core)
Error is tension between:
    - what the system assumes will recur
    - and what actually recurs under execution

5.2 — Bug
A bug is a hidden cut: an unintended distinction that redirects slices.

5.3 — Debugging
Debugging is controlled slicing:
    - locate the hidden cut
    - remove or reshape it
    - restore coherent recurrence
---
6 — COMPLEXITY AS RECURRENCE COST

6.1 — Time complexity
Time cost = number of slice steps required for identity to reach a target.

6.2 — Space complexity
Space cost = how many boundaries must remain stable at once.

6.3 — Hard problems (structural view)
“Hard” means: the system cannot keep enough boundaries stable simultaneously to guarantee collapse into a single efficient continuation.

Complexity is coherence pressure under constraint.
---
7 — INFORMATION, COMPRESSION, AND GENERALIZATION

7.1 — Compression
Compression is reducing the number of distinctions needed to preserve identity-relevant structure.

7.2 — Generalization
Generalization is a boundary that survives many slices across many contexts.

7.3 — Overfitting
Overfitting is a boundary that only survives inside the training slice chain, then collapses elsewhere.
---
8 — MACHINE LEARNING AS RECURRENCE SCULPTING

8.1 — Training
Training is tension-driven boundary reshaping:
    - keep updates that reduce mismatch
    - discard updates that increase mismatch

8.2 — Loss
Loss is explicit tension: a numeric proxy for “this identity is failing to preserve itself.”

8.3 — Model
A model is a learned fabric of boundaries that predicts which slices will remain coherent.

8.4 — Inference
Inference is fast traversal through the learned fabric to select a continuation.
---
9 — THE COMPUTATIONAL TRANSLATION TABLE

Cut → predicate / classification / decision
Boundary → data structure / type / constraint
Tension → loss / error signal / inconsistency pressure
Slice → state transition / update step
Recurrence → loop / function / repeated rule
Fabric → program architecture / system topology
Dimension → feature axis stable under transformations
Identity → invariant / stable representation
Agency → control policy selecting updates

## Chunk 7 — VALUE, ETHICS, AND MORALITY AS TENSION GOVERNANCE (v4.0) <a id="chunk-7"></a>
1 — WHY “VALUE” IS IN-SCOPE

If the canon claims to unify structure across domains,
it must explain why systems prefer some continuations over others.

“Preference” is not mystical.
It is coherence management.

Value is how an identity ranks slice options.
---
2 — THE MINIMAL DEFINITION OF VALUE

Value = expected coherence outcome.

A system values a continuation when it:
    - reduces destructive tension
    - preserves identity
    - increases stable recurrence
    - expands viable future slices

Disvalue is the opposite:
    - tension that breaks invariants
    - recurrence that destabilizes identity
    - slices that reduce future option-space

Value is not decoration.
Value is the navigation metric of agency.
---
3 — GOOD / BAD WITHOUT MORALIZING

Good = coherence gain (for the identity in question).
Bad = coherence loss (for the identity in question).

This is not “everything is relative.”
It’s structural:
you must specify the identity scope.

The moment you choose a scope, value becomes measurable in principle:
    - stability
    - resilience under slicing
    - tolerance to perturbation
